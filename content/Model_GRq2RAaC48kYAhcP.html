 ARA : Term

ARA (ARA) is an Application that supports NCI approval process for CSR unsolicited Application (required mostly for those requesting high funding).

I2E Application

 

https://eRA.NIH.gov/NIH_and_grantor_agencies/other/awaiting_receipt_of_Application.cfm 

The main role of the Awaiting Receipt of Application (ARA) module is to electronically track and link information received in advance of submission to a Application, after the Application is received or logged into the eRA system. For instance, an IC may be willing to accept a Application that is at or over the $500K limit of Direct Costs in any year. The IC uses an ARA form to electronically inform CSR's DRR (DRR) of this concurrence. DRR staff will then match the ARA request from the IC to the right Application and eRA will automatically inform the IC.

ARA also facilitates requests for assignments from ICs and other information that needs to be connected to a specific Application.

The new ARA replaces the manual paper-based process that has been used to match information to Application.

 RFA : Term

An RFA is a formal statement that solicits Grant or Cooperative Agreement Application in a well-defined scientific R15 to accomplish specific CAA objectives. An RFA indicates the estimated amount of funds set aside for the competition, the estimated number of awards to be made, whether cost sharing is required, and the Application submission date(s). For cooperative agreements, the RFA will describe the responsibilities and obligations of NIH and awardees as well as joint responsibilities and obligations. Application submitted in response to an RFA are usually reviewed by a SRG (SRG) specially convened by the awarding component that issued the RFA.

 

Request for Application (RFA)

  • Used to stimulate research in a priority R15.
  • Usually involves a single Application receipt date.
  • Allows use of standard plus special review criteria.
  • Has money committed up-front (i.e., “set-aside”).

 

NCI participation on another IC’s RFA requires additional approvals

  • NCI participation on another IC’s RFA, when the set-aside commitment is $1M or less, requires:
    • Initiation by PO;
    • Approval by Division Director; and
    • Approval by the SPL and then by the Director.
  • NCI participation on another IC’s RFA, when the set-aside commitment will be >$1M, requires:
    • Initiation by PO;
    • Approval by Division Director; and
    • Approval by NCI SPL and, if >$2M, by BSA.

 SRG : Term

A SRG is a FACA-chartered committee or SEP; performs R29 level of review (CSR and NCI).

 

A Peer Review committee group of primarily non-government experts (peer reviewers), qualified by training or experience in particular scientific or technical fields, or as authorities knowledgeable in the various disciplines and fields related to the Application under review, to evaluate and give expert advice on the scientific and technical merit of the Application. No more than one-fourth of the members of any SRG may be Federal employees, as noted in 42 CFR 52(h).

 

The R29 level of a two-stage Peer Review system. These legislatively mandated panels of subject matter experts are established according to scientific discipline or medical specialty. They are a component part of an IRG (IRG) that advises on the scientific and technical merit of research Application. Also known as SRG.

 NFRP : Term

NFRP, a web Application designed to find NCI funded research Grant, Contract awards, and Intramural Research projects.

 

https://fundedresearch.cancer.gov/nciportfolio/ 

 

NFRP8 is managed by CSSI/KMSP and reports on NCI projects and NCI Research Coding. NFRP data are considered by the NCI as Official Public Data. Primary groups that supply data to NFRP are: RAEB (Extramural Grant and Research Contracts); DCEG (Intramural projects); CCR (Intramural projects); and, OBF (RMS and Leidos projects).

At the end of each FY, the Data Collection Process for NFRP is as follows:

 

  1. RAEB images final FY data and transfers it to an NFRP schema in I2E.
  2. KMSP accesses that data and runs scripts to load/copy it into the NFRP database.
  3. OBF collects data from Intramural groups and sends it via spreadsheets to KMSP.
  4. KMSP runs scripts to load the Intramural data to the NFRP database.
  5. OBF sends RMS and Leidos data to KMSP via spreadsheets.
  6. KMSP runs scripts to load the RMS and Leidos data to the NFRP database.

 

KMSP and OBF work closely during each new FY’s Data Validation Process. KMSP performs the R29 level QA-QC of NFRP data as it is being loaded to the NFRP database. OBF validates and approves project and disease category data before that data is made public on NFRP. The total NCI Budget is validated by OBF as the ‘Cancer’ category on NFRP. During the validation process, OBF staff have access to the NFRP development system where data are loaded as they are received. They can use the development system as an automated aid in their validation process. In addition, KMSP provides reports of the working FY data for OBF to validate.

 Carryover : Term 		As indicated by the NoA (NoA), Carryover authority provides Recipient permission to carry over funds unobligated at the end of a Budget Period to the next Budget Period. For awards under the Streamlined Non-Competing Award Process (SNAP), funds are automatically carried over and are available for expenditure during the entire Project Period. However, under those awards, the Recipient will be required to indicate, as part of its non-competing continuation request, whether its estimated un-obligated balance (including prior year Carryover) is expected to be greater than 25 percent of the current year’s total Budget as well as provide a dollar estimate of the Unobligated Balance.  Score : Term

The NIH utilizes a 9-point rating scale (1 = exceptional; 9 = poor) for all Application; the same scale is used for overall impact scores and for criterion scores (Score Guidance).

Before the SRG meeting, each reviewer assigned to an Application gives a separate Score for each of (at least) five review criteria (i.e., Significance, Investigator(s), Innovation, Approach, and Environment for research Grant and cooperative agreements; see Review Criteria at a Glance). For all Application the individual scores of the assigned reviewers and discussant(s) for these criteria are reported to the Applicant.

 

In addition, each reviewer assigned to an Application gives a preliminary overall impact Score for that Application. In many review meetings, the preliminary scores are used to determine which Application will be discussed in full at the meeting. For each Application that is discussed at the meeting, a final impact Score is given by each eligible committee member (without conflicts of interest) including the assigned reviewers. Each member's Score reflects his/her evaluation of the overall impact that the project is likely to have on the research field(s) involved.

 

The final overall impact Score for each discussed Application is determined by calculating the mean Score from all the eligible members' final impact scores, and multiplying the average by 10; the final overall impact Score is reported on the Summary Statement. Thus, the final overall impact scores range from 10 (high impact) through 90 (low impact). Numerical impact scores are not reported for Application that are not discussed (ND), which may be reported as ++ on the face page of the Summary Statement and typically rank in the bottom half of the Application.

 

Applicants just receiving their scores or Summary Statement should consult our Next Steps page for detailed guidance. Applicants seeking advice beyond that available online may want to contact the NIH PO listed at the top of the Summary Statement.

 

An Application may be designated NRFC (NRFC) by the SRG if it lacks significant and substantial merit; presents serious ethical problems in the protection of human subjects from research risks; or presents serious ethical problems in the use of vertebrate animals, biohazards, and/or select agents. Application designated as NRFC do not proceed to the second level of Peer Review (National Council Council/Council) because they cannot be funded.

 Assignment : Term

HOW Application ASSIGNMENTS ARE MADE

 

The DRR (DRR) in the CSR (CSR) assigns each Application to a review group with the expertise to evaluate the scientific and technical merit of the Application and to one or more Institutes/Centers (ICs) for funding consideration.

 

While many NIH policies give authority to the DRR to determine assignments, staff of the DRR consult with ICs, Peer Review Officers (SRO) and IRG (IRG) Chiefs and applicants to reach the most appropriate Assignment. How much of the Application is read in making an Assignment? The honest answer is as much of the Application as is needed to make the determination. Referral staff have access to the entire Application. In many cases, they concentrate on the Abstract and Specific Aims in making an Assignment, with attention also paid to the Research Strategy. Requests made by investigators and the Assignment of previous Application are also considered. Referral staff regularly discuss the Assignment of Application and how to handle unusual situations.

 

The Assignment of a Application involves a series of decisions:

 

  • An Institute or Center (IC) is identified for primary Assignment for funding consideration. This determination is based on the focus and mission of each of the twenty-four Institutes and Centers of NIH. Due to the increasingly multidisciplinary nature of scientific inquiry, the complex biological problems being addressed, and the use of many common research methodologies the ICs share many common interests. The interests of the ICs are described on the main NIH Web SITE. Assignments are limited to the ICs that participate in the specific FOA used for Application submission.
  • Application may also receive dual IC assignments. Dual assignments acknowledge the shared interests of ICs in a topic and make all appropriate ICs aware of the Application. The primary Assignment is reflected in the Assignment number (CA for the NCI, AG for the National Institute on Aging, etc.). When multiple dual assignments are made, a rank order (secondary, tertiary, etc.) is not established. Both the primary and dual ICs have access to the Application and Summary Statement and Council consideration is given by both the primary and dual ICs. However, a dual Assignment does not necessarily increase the chance of an Award. The frequency of a dual Assignment leading to a change to primary and Award is less than 2%.
  • Finally, the Application is assigned for review to the CSR or to one of the other IC review groups. CSR reviews most R01s, fellowships, and Small Business Application. IC review groups handle Application that have institute-specific features such as CAA projects, training Grant, career development awards, and many responses to Requests for Application.

 

For Application assigned to an IC review branch for Peer Review, a general Assignment is made to that IC; the staff in the review unit subsequently decides whether the Application is to be reviewed by an IC standing committee or by an IC SEP.

 

For Application to be reviewed within CSR, a two-stage process is employed with initial Assignment to the IRG (IRG, a cluster of scientifically related study sections) level and subsequent Assignment to a specific SRG or SEP. By assigning all Application to the IRG level rather than directly to an individual SRG, the IRG Chief and the SRO within the IRG have the opportunity to gain a broad perspective of the areas of science covered by their IRG and to appreciate changes in emphasis and the emergence of new areas. A number of methods are used to determine assignments within the IRG, though all involve discussions among the SRO and the Chief. Finally, the IRG also have the option of suggesting that the Application is more appropriate for a different IRG; they may discuss this with other SRO or IRG Chiefs or return the Application to the DRR for reassignment.

 

Note: The terms SRG and SRG (SRG) are normally used for continuing review groups in CSR and the ICs, respectively. These are groups with members who have been appointed for multi-year terms of service; at any given meeting there are also usually temporary members present to provide any additional expertise needed. Special Emphasis Panels (SEPs) are review groups formed on an ad hoc basis to review Application requiring special expertise or when a conflict of interest situation occurs.

 FACA : Term

The FACA (FACA) (Pub.L. 92–463, 86 Stat. 770, enacted October 6, 1972), is a United States federal law which governs the behavior of federal Council committees. In particular, it has special emphasis on open meetings, chartering, public involvement, and reporting.

 

https://www.gsa.gov/policy-regulations/policy/federal-Council-committee-act-FACA-management-overview

 

The FACA was enacted in 1972 to ensure that advice by the various Council committees formed over the years is objective and accessible to the public. The Act formalized a process for establishing, operating, overseeing, and terminating these Council bodies and created the Committee Management System Secretariat to monitor compliance with the Act.

In 1976, Executive Order 12024 delegated to the administrator of GSA all responsibilities of the president for implementing the FACA (FACA). Secretariat operations are directed at reporting to the president and Congress on the activities of at least 1000 federal Council committees. Samples of documents and forms used by the Secretariat and its clients can be found in the "Advice and Guidance" pages.

Executive orders and congressional revisions have further refined the extension and the Application of the Act in 1993, 1997, and 1998, and the extent and nature of the Secretariat's reporting of the activities of the committees. The legal history is easy to follow in the "Legislation and Regulation" pages.

 

www.gsa.gov/FACA

 CTRP : Term

CTRP maintains a comprehensive database of information on all NCI-supported interventional clinical trials open to accrual as of January 1, 2009. This database, also referred to as CTRP, helps identify gaps and duplicate studies in clinical research, facilitates clinical trial prioritization, and standardizes trial data capture and sharing.

Key benefits of CTRP include:

  • Standardized abstraction of protocol information
  • Consistent terminology and Coding to optimize search and retrieval of cancer trials information
  • Biomarker and patient-level accrual data
  • Standardized person and organization data elements

 FOIA : Term

The basic function of the FOIA is to ensure informed citizens, vital to the functioning of a democratic society.

 

Since 1967, the FOIA (FOIA) has provided the public the right to request access to records from any federal agency. It is often described as the law that keeps citizens in the know about their government. Federal agencies are required to disclose any information requested under the FOIA unless it falls under one of nine exemptions which protect interests such as personal privacy, national security, and law enforcement.
